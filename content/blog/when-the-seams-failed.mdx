    This is fiction, but the implementation gap it points to is real. New Zealand's current Digital Target State explicitly points toward shared digital public infrastructure, common platforms, secure credentials, standardisation, and treating digital resources as national assets. The harder question is whether those ideas are becoming operationally resilient quickly enough, with enough fallback, assurance, and stewardship, to hold under real pressure. The legislative direction also already exists in part: the Customer and Product Data Act 2025 sets a model around valid requests, mandatory electronic systems, and technical and performance requirements, while Privacy Act mechanisms such as approved information sharing agreements and IPP 12 show that governed sharing and controlled offshore disclosure are matters of discipline and safeguards, not simply a blanket prohibition.

# When the Seams Failed

Mereana had done this before. Open the link. Confirm the address. Check the income line. Submit.
Done in two minutes, maybe three if the phone was being slow.

That was what she expected when the message came through just after eight. Her youngest had been coughing most of the night.
The kitchen still smelled faintly of toast and Vicks. One school lunch sat half-made on the bench. Shoes by the door. Phone on charge.
The power bill tucked under the fruit bowl, where she put things she had not forgotten, exactly, but did not want to look at again just yet.

The message looked routine enough not to worry about. Household details. Confirm your information to avoid interruption. Blue button. Standard wording. She tapped it while waiting for the kettle.

The page then asked her to verify herself. She did. It asked again. Then it stopped and told her another agency could not confirm the information right now.

She stared at the screen, annoyed more than worried. Government sites did this sometimes. Refresh. Try again. Swear at the phone. Move on.
From the hallway, the youngest yelled that they could not find the other shoe.

Her mind was still on the spinning page, but the answer came out of her at once, quick with habit and alertness.

“Under the couch,” she called back, already listening for the thud of small feet turning toward the lounge, and hit refresh.

This time it got further. Address confirmed. Income line pending. Then the wheel started turning again, and a different message appeared.

'We’re unable to retrieve information from another service right now. Please try again later.'

That was when the day shifted. Not because it looked like much. Because she knew the type of message. The kind that could leave you stuck for hours between one system and another while no one told you anything useful.

By 8:40 she had tried three times and still was not sure whether the update had partly gone through, sent multiple times, failed completely, or disappeared into whatever sat between one system and another.

She told herself it could wait until she found five quiet minutes somewhere in the morning, though she already knew the kind of day that rarely gave them up.

That was what she told herself on the drive to work too, while the kids argued in the back seat and she mentally moved money around that had not arrived yet.
If the update cleared today, things would be tight but manageable.
If it slipped, she would need to stretch something else. Groceries. Petrol.
The power bill under the fruit bowl. One of them would wait.

At work, the mood was off in a way she could not place at first. Not panic. Just more irritation than usual, and not all for the same reason.
Someone in the office could not get a registration update through. Someone else was trying to sort a prescription issue. One of the guys in payroll was muttering about a government verification process taking twice as long as it should. A woman near the printer was trying to help her mother over the phone with some form that kept looping back to identity checks.

No one said anything grand about it. Just the usual lines people use when systems stop behaving.

“It’s being weird.”

“It says there’s no record, but there is.”

“It keeps sending me back.”

“They told me someone else has to confirm it first.”

Mereana sat with that for a bit longer than she meant to. It was one thing for her own update to be stuck. It was another to realise everyone seemed to be bumping into the same kind of wall.

---

At 9:19am, three blocks away and several layers above the kind of life Mereana was trying to hold together, the Government Chief Digital Officer joined a cross-agency call already in progress.

A referral that should have moved cleanly had stalled somewhere between send and receive. Routine checks were coming back partial, delayed, or oddly empty. Queues were beginning to build behind dependencies that were still technically available, but not consistently enough to trust.

Nothing had failed in a way that gave the room something clean to point at. The trouble sat in the handoff, in the stretch between one system saying yes and another being able to use it. At first glance it looked narrow. Under analysis, it kept refusing to stay that way. No one called it that yet.

The first half hour was spent doing what official rooms always did first.
Narrowing. Containing. Sorting symptoms into categories that made them feel local.

“Could be latency offshore.”

“Then why are some of the calls clearing?”

“We’ve got acknowledgements in one place and blanks in another.”

“Identity is involved, but it doesn’t stop there.”

“It looks like a handoff problem.”

That sat in the air for a moment, then was handled the way awkward things usually were in official rooms: by being reclassified.

“Or a routing issue.”

“Or provider behaviour.”

“Or timing between them.”

The GCDO listened without saying much. He knew this moment: the one where people kept reaching for narrower explanations because the wider one would force decisions no one was ready to make. Not denial, exactly. More the bureaucratic habit of turning an emerging systemic problem back into a set of manageable components.

Then someone asked, “If the offshore path doesn’t recover, what can we actually rely on here?”

The silence after that was longer than it should have been.

One person spoke first, but only to make the question smaller.
“Depends what stage the transaction is at.”
Another voice came in.
“Depends which part already thinks it has finished.”
A third added, “Depends how many other processes lean on the same confirmation without us realising.”

That was answer enough.

---

Mereana called during her break.

The queue message thanked her for her patience before she had agreed to be patient. The hold music was cheerful in the irritating way hold music often is, as if brightness might count as reassurance.
She stood in the lunchroom while the microwave counted down someone else’s leftovers and the queue position did not seem to move.

When she finally got through, the woman on the line sounded kind and tired, like someone who had been having versions of the same conversation all morning.

“I’m sorry,” she said first. “Yes, there are delays.”
“Some linked systems are under pressure at the moment.”
“I can’t see which agency hasn’t responded from this side.”
“I wouldn’t resubmit unless it fails completely. I don’t want to make it worse for you.”

Mereana looked down at the message on her phone. It was the kind of answer that left you exactly where you were.

“It affects the payment, doesn’t it?” she asked.
There was a pause.
“It might,” the woman said. “I’m sorry. I just can’t say yet.”

That was when it stopped being an inconvenience. After the call she sat with the phone face down on the table for a moment.

It was not that she expected systems to be perfect. Nobody did. But there was a difference between a delay and not knowing who held the answer.

---

By early afternoon, the drag had spread beyond whichever agencies were first to notice it.

Payroll teams delayed anything that relied on external confirmation. Borderline cases were kicked into manual review. Support lines thickened with people caught between their own systems and public-sector processes that no longer settled cleanly.
A truck sat too long at a depot because a record could not be confirmed quickly enough. A parent put off picking up medication because the support tied to it had slipped into review. A worker in one agency typed notes she suspected another agency would not be able to see until tomorrow.
Nothing stopped. It just all took longer.

---

Internally, people had stopped treating it like a narrow outage.

The language in the briefings got sharper: international connectivity issues, changing provider behaviour, fragmented implementation, weak local fallback. The GCDO read one draft summary, then another. Too tidy. Too safe. Too much language designed to sound managed.

None of it was false. That was part of the problem. Each phrase described a portion of what was happening. Put together, they still found a way to leave the shape of it blurred.

A principal adviser sent through another line for the morning briefing.
New Zealand’s digital services remain operational, but confidence in the handoffs between them has dropped under pressure.
He read it twice. That was closer to the truth than anything else he had seen that day. Not because it sounded good. Because it sounded plain enough to be real.

At 2:16pm the minister’s office asked for something usable. Not a full briefing. A line. Something they could say publicly without opening five more questions.
He sent the sentence through as written.
Three minutes later the reply came back.
_Can we make “confidence in the handoffs” softer?_
He looked at the screen for a moment, then locked his phone.

---

By mid-afternoon, Mereana had checked hers six times in an hour.

Nothing useful. One message told her the update was still being processed. Another told her she did not need to take any further action at this time.

That last line annoyed her more than she expected. Waiting was one thing when you had room for it. It was another when the week was already spoken for.

She opened her banking app. Closed it again. Opened the message once more as if it might have changed.
It hadn’t.

At the end of the day she sat in the car for a minute before starting it, doing the maths again.

If the payment landed, they would be fine. Tight, but fine.

If it slipped, she would need to move something else. Ask for more time. Hold something off another few days. Ring her sister. Try not to make the stress visible in front of the kids.

---

At a red light on the way home, Mereana checked her phone again.

A new message had come through.

Your update has been received and may take longer than usual to process. You do not need to take any further action at this time.

She let out a short laugh. Nothing about the day had suggested no further action to her.

Behind her, someone tapped the horn. The lights had gone green.

She put the phone down and drove on.
