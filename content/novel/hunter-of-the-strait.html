Chapter Chapter 1

The interrogation room smelled of damp wool, old cigarettes and, beneath them both, wet stone — the close, stale smell of a place that never fully dried. It was the smell of a room in which many people had been asked difficult questions, and the questions had accumulated in the walls.

Cooney had been a policeman for twenty-three years, and in those years he had developed the specific professional patience of a man who knew that the most important thing he could do in a room was wait. He was waiting now, with his hands flat on the metal table and his face carrying the particular quality of a man who has heard the story he has been given and knows it is not the story. He was not unsympathetic. He was not stupid. He was trying, with genuine effort, to make the story he had been given work, and it kept failing its structural tests.

"Tell me again," he said. "From the beginning."

Benton Bright had been in this room for two hours. He had told the story three times. He would be telling it a fourth time now, and the fourth time would be the same as the third and the second and the first, because the only changes available to it were the kind that would make the story worse rather than better and he was not prepared to make them. He had made a decision, sitting in the hospital waiting room two days after the cave, with the scar on his shoulder still being changed every six hours: the story he told in this room was not the complete story. The complete story would not survive the telling. The complete story required Cooney to believe in things he was professionally obligated not to believe in, and Benton respected Cooney too much to do that to him.

"The Philosophical Society held a private event at the property on Te Aro stream," Benton said. "Myself and two associates were on the property investigating. We were attacked by members of the Society. In the resulting incident, the building was structurally damaged. Several Society members were killed or are unaccounted for. Miss Gus was injured and has been admitted to Porirua Hospital for treatment. Mr. Spöring was separately injured and is also in hospital." He paused. "That is what happened."

Cooney looked at him. He had the face of a man containing something with considerable discipline. "Bright. I have known you for eleven years. You tell me that two months ago."

"That is what happened."

A pause. Cooney moved one hand from the table and pressed it to the back of his neck and looked at the ceiling. He was doing the specific calculation that Benton had been watching him do intermittently throughout the afternoon — weighing what he believed against what he could professionally afford to believe, in the knowledge that the two were different quantities and the difference between them was currently larger than usual. He had been to the site. He had seen the collapse, the water line in the valley, the specific texture of the damage. He had read the constables' reports. He was a thorough man and the reports had been thorough, and nowhere in their thoroughness had they produced an event that accounted for what Cooney had seen.

"Three Society members are missing," he said. "Not dead. Missing. Including Balász."

"I know."

"You were there."

"I was there."

Cooney looked at him directly. It was a long look — the look of a man saying, without words, that he understands the terms of the transaction being proposed and has decided to accept them, because the alternative is a conversation that neither of them will come out of well. "The chief wants me off it," he said.

"I heard."

"Three concurrent investigations in the last two years, all three of which touched on the Society in some form. The first two went cold at the same desk. This one will too." He folded his hands. "I am telling you this because I knew your father. And because I am a thorough man and I like to close my files, and this one I cannot close, and I would like you to understand exactly how clearly I see the outline of what you're choosing not to tell me." He uncuffed him. "Keep your head down, Benton. Don't leave town for a while."

Benton stood. He picked up his coat. He looked at Cooney for a moment.

"Thank you," he said. It was not for anything specific. Cooney understood that.

He let himself out.

ONE YEAR LATER

The print machine gave the rotary press the particular sound of controlled consequence: the rhythmic iron authority of ink being pressed against paper, meaning being multiplied, the story being made real in the only way that mattered to the version of Benton Bright who had rebuilt himself on this foundation after everything else had been removed. He had been running the Truth Papers for thirteen months. Each issue was an argument with the official account of what had happened under Te Aro. He did not expect to win it. He expected to have it on the record.

He cut the current run at the table — the blade clean through two hundred copies, the stack precisely aligned, the cut precise. This was the kind of work that asked nothing of him except mechanical attention, and mechanical attention was the mode he occupied most comfortably now. The press required his hands. His hands did not know anything about the thing Marcus kept in a deed-box or the way the dreams had been specific and incremental for a year. The blade was the blade. The paper was the paper. The story was printed and cut and tied and real.

The scar on his shoulder was two years old. He had given it the same accounting he gave everything: it was there, it was documented, it was part of the record. He did not examine the question of what exactly had made it — the ceremonial blade on the bridge had made it, the curved edge scraping against bone before finding the gap between muscle and sinew, and the memory of the cold of it on a warm night was one of the cleaner ones he had retained from that evening, compared to what came after. On humid mornings it produced a phantom ache that reminded him of provenance. He pressed two fingers to it as a reflex, a grounding mechanism, the way you press your feet to a floor to confirm the floor is real. The ache answered, as it always did.

Dr. Harvey George's compound kept the rest of it manageable. Lithium bromide and Veronal in a ratio they had adjusted three times on the basis of what constituted functional without chemically incapacitated — a threshold that had proven more moveable than either of them would have anticipated. Benton took the capsules three times daily and they suppressed the worst of the intrusions, kept the tremors in the hands to imperceptible, kept the voice in the dark water of sleep from becoming the loudest thing in his awareness. He had not told Harvey about the dreams' specific content. He had not told Harvey about the figure at the foot of the bed that had appeared six times in the previous month. Harvey was a thorough man and a thoroughman, given the complete picture, would have taken a position on continued capacity to work that Benton could not currently afford.

"Ben!" Mr. Cain's voice came up from the floor below, over the last mechanical gasping of the press. "Count's done."

Benton set the blade down and went to the window. Below, on the Te Aro street, the morning was doing its Wednesday business: a man with a bread delivery, a motor car moving with the irritated deliberateness of an urban vehicle negotiating a street built before urban vehicles were the primary form of conveyance. The newsboy at the corner already had his Tuesday edition and was calling it with the particular public-address quality that indicated he had at least two stories he considered worth the effort. Benton could not hear the words. From this height and angle, the boy was a small figure with a voice extending further than his body justified.

He took a capsule from his coat pocket and swallowed it with the last of yesterday's cold tea.

Mrs. Cain appeared at the workroom door with a tray at eleven o'clock, as she always did, with the determination of a woman who had decided that the daily tea was a structure that was going to hold regardless of what else was happening. She was younger than her husband by fifteen years and had the particular quality of a person who has chosen to find the press's periodic chaos amusing rather than distressing, which was a choice Benton found he respected more the longer he worked above them. She set down the tray — Earl Grey, coffee cake, a small vase of something that had been purchased with no expectation of comment though comment was probably hoped for — and looked at the cut stack on the table.

"Good run?" she said.

"Fine run," Benton said.

"I haven't read it." She poured. "I expect it's fine."

"It's fine."

Mr. Cain appeared at the workroom door behind her, wiping his hands on a cloth in the manner of a man completing a prior task before beginning a new one. He had operated the print shop for thirty years and had the specific quality of a man whose entire professional identity was invested in its machinery and who found the machinery's periodical use for activities he found personally alarming somewhat beside the point. His opinion of the Truth Papers had never been stated and would not need to be. His manner communicated it with complete economy. He drank his tea. He did not comment on the current edition. He finished the cake. He went back downstairs.

Benton ate his cake and thought about Marcus and Pid at Porirua. The visits were monthly. Marcus had been deteriorating last time — not from whatever the hospital was treating, which was what the hospital called what Marcus had — but from the specific institutional weight of being a man who knew exactly what was wrong and was not allowed to say so. His eyes had been too careful. His conversations had been too precise. He was managing his account of things, and the management was costing him. Benton had stopped trying to address this directly. He brought books. Marcus wanted books. Books were an available form of help and he provided them.

Pid he had not seen in three months. The last visit had been in September, when she had been stable, recovering, specific in her account of what she needed to recover from and what the hospital could offer toward that. She had looked directly at him in the way that Pid always looked directly at him — which was a quality he had noticed early in their professional relationship and valued, the specific quality of a person who decided in the first twenty seconds of any conversation what level of honesty was appropriate and then maintained that level without further calculation. She had looked at him in September and said: I'll be out by December. It won't be from this door. He had not been certain what she meant.

He finished the cake. He washed the cup in the corner sink by reflex. He put on his coat.

It was the footstep on the top stair that he noticed first — not Mrs. Cain's, which he knew from two years of shared walls to follow a particular rhythm, and not Mr. Cain's, which had a slight drag to it from the bad hip. These were measured and patient and paused at the door without knocking.

The door opened without invitation.

The man was in his late forties in a grey wool suit that had not been made by any tailor whose work Benton had seen on a Wellington street — too precisely fitted, too indifferent to the local preference for heavy brown wool. He carried a briefcase as a man carries a document rather than a tool: handle held just clear of his leg, as though its contents dictated the manner of its transport. He had a government officer's face — composed without being pleasant, attentive without being curious — and pale eyes that moved once around the room in a rapid, complete inventory before settling on Benton with the focus of a man arriving at the main item on an internal list.

"Mr. Bright." Not a question. He closed the door behind him. "I apologise for the intrusion. Francis Stanhope, Ministry of Internal Affairs, publications and licensing. I wonder if I might have five minutes."

"I'm finishing a print run," Benton said. He kept cutting.

Stanhope set his briefcase on the far end of the cutting table — not close to the stacked papers, not rudely distant, a carefully neutral position — and opened it. He removed a manila folder, placed it on the table without opening it, and folded his hands. He had the practised stillness of someone for whom silence in a room served a calculable function.

"You hold a printer's licence," he said, "registered to this address under Mr. Cain's auspices. A printer's licence covers the commercial operation of a press. It does not cover the publication and distribution of periodical literature for public consumption. The Truth Papers, in its current scope and circulation, falls under a different instrument of oversight entirely." He paused. "I mention this not as a formal notice — not today — but as context for a conversation I believe would be useful to both of us."

Benton set down the cutting blade. He looked at Stanhope rather than at the folder. "Who told you about it."

"The ministry monitors publications operating in the grey areas of the licensing framework. It is a routine function." He opened the folder. Inside, Benton could see a copy of the previous issue of the Truth Papers, annotated in blue ink, both neat and thorough. Beside it, a copy of today's edition. Still visibly damp from the press — he could see the faint warp of paper that fresh ink on a humid morning produced. It had not left the building yet.

He looked at the copy and did not say anything about it. He thought several things carefully and kept them.

"The claims in your last three issues touch on areas requiring careful management," Stanhope continued. "The Philosophical Society's premises were administratively condemned following a structural incident thirteen months ago. Three associated individuals remain listed as missing persons. There is no active criminal file." He turned a page. "The photograph in this issue — which purports to show a human subject exhibiting non-human physiological characteristics — falls under Section Seven of the 1916 Act. Material likely to disturb public confidence in institutional order." He closed the folder with the same precision with which he had opened it. "I want to be direct. I am not here to prevent journalism. I am here to clarify that the licensing question is real, that it will require formal address, and that the ministry would find it useful to understand your intentions. Given that understanding, the formal process might resolve itself quietly." He lifted the briefcase from the table. "Without it — a hearing, some disruption, neither of us needs. That is all."

He moved to the door.

"That copy," Benton said.

Stanhope stopped.

"Today's edition. The run finished forty minutes ago." Benton kept his voice level. "It hasn't been distributed. How did you come to have it?"

Something crossed Stanhope's face — brief, precise, and quickly managed, the adjustment of a man who has received information he had not anticipated and is filing it rapidly. "A colleague sourced it. Routine observation of the print operation."

"What colleague."

"Good day, Mr. Bright."

He went down the stairs at the same steady pace. Below, the front door opened and closed. Benton went to the window in time to see Stanhope cross the Te Aro street and open the door of a dark motor car idling at the far kerb — not a government vehicle, nothing marked, nothing official in any visible way. It pulled into the traffic and moved north before he could read the registration. He tried for the last three letters. He got two of them and lost the third.

He stood at the window for a moment. Then he returned to the cutting table and stood with his palms flat on the stack of newsprint.

Forty minutes since the run finished. His copies were here. The stands were not yet stocked. Stanhope had produced the copy from a pre-loaded folder — which meant someone had provided it to the Ministry before the man left for this address. Which meant someone with access to this building, and to the press, had read it off the first run and passed it on.

There were two people with keys to the front door besides himself.

He set that thought carefully aside and picked up the blade.

Downstairs, Mr. Cain had gone very quiet.

"I'll have the rent settled by Friday," Benton called.

No reply came back.

The bundling was the last part of the production he actually liked — the blade through the stack, clean through a hundred sheets at once, the satisfying thud of it, the precise alignment of the cut edges. He counted the stacks and secured them with twine.

He held one back and looked at the photograph on the front page for the last time before he stopped being able to look at it the way a reader would.

It was imperfect as evidence went: his fingers had been in the frame, the lower corners obscured, the angle oblique, the flash giving everything the flat stark quality of an impact rather than a document. But there was enough. The figure on the ground. The fluid pooled beneath it — dark, viscous, almost black in the print. The specific absence of anything in its features that corresponded to a human face. Pid had put it down from thirty feet and the result was not a dead man; it was the evidence of something for which dead was the nearest available word. He had looked at this photograph two hundred times in the course of laying the issue and it had not become less strange. If anything the reproduction had made it worse — the newsprint grain giving the thing on the ground a quality of texture it had not had in the original print.

He set the copy face-down on the stack.

It took him under half an hour to load the truck — Mr. Cain's Ford pickup, borrowed with the wordlessness of an arrangement that had long since graduated from requests. The engine turned with its characteristic low reluctance, the note of something that had been reliable for long enough to resent being asked. He came up through the Te Aro valley in the turn-of-day traffic, stacks in the back, and joined the queue on Lambton Quay behind a flour delivery already regretting its choice of route.

The queue backed up at the intersection. A truck had stalled on the tram line. A newsboy was working the jam on the footpath side — fourteen, perhaps, with the practised economy of a boy who has turned a street corner into an office. He moved between the stalled bonnets with his bundle of afternoon editions tucked under one arm, calling without any particular urgency: Parliament silent on Rātana petition — fourteen thousand signatures, no reply from the Crown! A flour merchant in the vehicle ahead bought a copy without looking up from his wheel. A woman with shopping bags did not. The boy moved on, his voice diminishing into the grid of horns and engines with the ease of something the city had learned to absorb.

Benton kept his hands on the wheel. The traffic did not move. He waited.

Then, at the back of his awareness, a smell.

He noticed it at the edge of conscious thought — the way you notice a sound in another room before deciding it warrants attention. Salt, and beneath that the specific marine organic smell of deep rock pools at lowest tide, of things not designed for surface air. Wellington harbour was visible to his right where the streets opened toward the waterfront. Smells came off the harbour. That was a rational explanation and he held it for approximately forty seconds.

The smell did not diminish with distance. It intensified.

He tightened his grip on the wheel. The traffic ahead had not moved. The newsboy's voice was gone. The ordinary sounds of the intersection — the other motors, the dray horse at the kerb, a woman with shopping debating the price of a thing — seemed to be arriving from further away than they had been, attenuated, as if the air between him and the street had grown dense by imperceptible degrees.

"There is nothing there," he said, aloud, to no one. His voice in the cab was flat and ordinary. He said it again, more quietly, which was a worse sign.

A soft sound from the passenger seat. Not quite breathing — the mechanism of breathing, without the human quality of it. The wet work of something drawing air into structures not evolved for the purpose. He did not look. He kept his eyes on the traffic ahead and his hands on the wheel and thought, methodically, about the capsules in his inner pocket and whether he had taken too many or too few. Either was possible. Harvey had mentioned once, with the specific casualness of a man covering his own uncertainty, that the salts and the Veronal in combination could produce vivid perceptual events in a state of sustained fatigue. He was fatigued. He was always fatigued now. This was that.

The temperature in the cab dropped. Quick and absolute, the cold of a room whose window has been opened in winter — except the windows were closed, both of them, cranked shut against the harbour wind.

He looked.

The figure was in the rough proportion of a man — seated upright, forearms resting on the knees, the posture almost ordinary. But the material of it was wrong in a way that bypassed description entirely and arrived directly at revulsion. Grey, and not the grey of anything he had a category for: not the grey of ash or stone or fog but the grey of an internal organ viewed from outside its context, the grey of a thing that had never been near light and which light, now finding it, could not quite agree on. Its surface gathered light wetly, as though perpetually filmed with moisture, and had a slight translucency at the edges where it met the leather of the seat. No features that he could locate. The front of its head was a single uninterrupted surface.

It was looking at him. He could not have said how he knew this, with no eyes to track. He knew it the way you know a dark window is occupied.

"Not real," he said. He heard his own voice this time from a considerable distance.

Then from somewhere below the sound of the street — below the frequency of traffic and voices and the ordinary mechanical pulse of the city — a resonance arrived, not audible through the ears but felt in the chest and the jaw and the root of the teeth. He had heard it before. In the nights. In the place below consciousness where a voice lived that called him toward dark water. A single sustained note, as impersonal as the hum of transformers on a telegraph wire. Enormous and entirely indifferent to him in the specific way that very large things are indifferent — not hostile, not cruel, simply operating at a scale in which he did not register.

He understood, in the moment of that sound, that whatever was happening in the cab was not a symptom. It was a correspondence. Something receiving a signal and something nearby that was part of the signal, and him sitting between the two of them in Mr. Cain's Ford pickup on a Wednesday afternoon.

The creature turned its smooth head toward him.

Its surface — the featureless plane of its face — began to separate. Not violently, with none of the horrible drama of tearing, but with the calm sequential logic of something opening for a purpose. Petals was the wrong word and his mind produced it anyway. What opened was not a mouth in any conventional sense but an aperture with teeth in layered radial rows set back into a structure that had its own depth, the way a well has depth. The sound it produced built rapidly through frequencies human vocal structures could not produce, and at the midpoint of its ascending shriek a car horn sounded from outside —

He was on the road.

The traffic signal had gone green an unknown interval ago — a gap of one car length now separated him from the vehicle ahead, and the driver behind him was leaning on his horn with the sustained patience of a man who had been doing this for several seconds already. Benton raised his hand in acknowledgment and moved the truck through the intersection.

He drove one block. Two. At the third block he pulled to the left, set the brake, and sat.

His hands on the wheel were trembling. Not severely — the fine tremor of wire under tension, visible only if you looked for it. He pressed them flat on the leather and breathed steadily. Outside, the city continued in its complete indifference: a woman with a pram, a baker's boy, two clerks in animated disagreement about something sufficiently trivial for a public pavement. All of it present and ordinary and entirely uninformed by anything that had just occurred in this cab.

He reached into his inner pocket and counted the capsules into his palm. Three. The correct number for this time of day. He held them, put two back, took one, swallowed it dry, and sat watching the street until the trembling settled itself to nothing.

Just overworked, he told himself. The stress of the run. Harvey said fatigue was a factor. He said this several times, inwardly, and on the third repetition he almost believed the first part.

He released the brake and moved back into the traffic.

By late afternoon he was at the last drop — C.M. Brown's bookstall at the city end of Lambton Quay, where the smell of vanilla pipe tobacco and old newsprint constituted a fixed point in the geography of Benton's working week.

Charlie Brown was built the way certain old buildings are — capaciously, without apology, and with complete indifference to whether the load-bearing elements had been distributed well. He occupied the stall the way water occupies a glass: entirely, and to the edges. His voice preceded him by half a second — a sound like a large door opening on a cold hinge, all rough warmth and no smoothness whatsoever, affectionate and abraded in equal measure. Sugar cut with pepper. You got the sweetness, but it dragged; it had texture; it left something behind in the ear.

"Benton." He planted both hands on the counter and leaned forward, which was as close to standing as the space and his proportions permitted. "Is this the final issue?"

"My magnum opus," Benton said, placing the last bundles on the shelf.

"Any of the last one left?"

Charlie reached behind the counter and produced an envelope thick with its contents, dropping it between them.

"All gone. Here's your cut."

"Every copy?"

"Sold an entire stack to a single man," Charlie said, his voice dropping to the register he reserved for the interesting part. "Long grey beard, well kept — more to him than he looked. Determined to have the lot. Paid without negotiating." He tapped the envelope. "That's why it's so full."

Benton pocketed the envelope. "Who was he?"

"Didn't say. I didn't press." Charlie leaned back in the chair, which registered the redistribution with a sound of structural protest. "If we're lucky he'll come back for these too."

"I'm not in this for the money," Benton said.

"I know that," Charlie said, without particular feeling about it.

He puffed twice, reflectively. "Speaking of unusual customers — there was a young woman in, few days back. Dark-haired, olive-complected, the continental sort of look. Well turned out but in a hurry." He gestured vaguely in the direction of the waterfront. "Asked if I knew about passenger ships running the Strait. Said she wanted to get south quickly. Privately, she said — I remember that word specifically, because it's an odd way to put it. 'Privately.'"

Benton's hand went still on the counter.

"Didn't catch her name," Charlie continued, obliviously. "She bought a copy of your last issue and left smartly. Pretty girl. Foreign look about her." He tapped out his pipe against the counter edge. "I told her the RMS Tahiti was sailing this week. She's not a Strait ship normally — transpacific, runs out to Rarotonga then up to Vancouver. But a Canterbury wool consortium have chartered her for a repositioning run while she goes south for refit. One-off sailing. That's the sort of thing I keep an eye on." He patted the stack of shipping notices under the counter with the pride of a man whose profession keeps him better informed than most. "She seemed very interested in that."

"When exactly?" Benton said.

"Three days back, give or take. Why — you know her?"

Benton looked at the stack of newsprint in his hand and set it on the counter. "What was she wearing?"

Charlie puffed at his pipe with the expression of a man pleased to be consulted after a period of being merely useful. "Let me think. A canvas coat, good buttons, up to here — something practical about it, not fashionable, the sort of coat you keep because it does what you need it to do. Boots to the knee, dark leather, a stiff ankle." He produced the stub of a pencil and tapped it against the counter's edge in the way of a man whose memory operates better with his hands busy. "She had a bag. Canvas, worn, not the sort you pack for a pleasure trip."

"Anyone with her?"

"No. Solo." He brought the pencil to rest. "She had the manner of a woman who had been somewhere already and was heading somewhere else. Not starting out — mid-journey. You know the sort." He tapped his temple. "She didn't linger. Bought your paper — had the exact change ready, as if she'd already looked up the price. Asked about the Tahiti straight after, no preamble."

"Did she say why she wanted to go south."

"No. Only that she needed to — and privately, she kept that word. Odd emphasis." He studied Benton's face with the mild perplexity of a man watching someone else's weather develop. "Benton. You know her."

Benton said nothing. He was looking at the space on the counter where his bundle of papers had been. Three days ago, Pid Gus was still a documented patient at Porirua Hospital, under Dr. Holbrook's care. And three days ago, before he had known any of this, someone had sent her, or she had come herself, already carrying his paper, already knowing which issue to ask for, already — in Charlie's estimation — mid-journey.

Pid moved like that. She always had. The deliberate purposefulness of a woman who arrived at conclusions before other people had finished framing their questions, and acted without waiting for consensus. He had watched her do it in Marcus's bookshop those years ago — the letter from the hospital, the teacup rattling in her hand, and Pid not crying, not deliberating, already three moves ahead. He had liked it enormously then. What he felt standing at Charlie's counter was something more complicated, and he did not yet have the right word for it.

She had known to come here. She had known about the Tahiti. She had read his own paper and arrived at conclusions from it that had sent her south. All without him. Without telling him she was going. Without the conversation they had been, in some theoretical future, supposed to have.

He was not entitled to whatever this feeling was. He was clear on that.

"She said one other thing," Charlie said, reaching back into some back parlour of his memory. "When I gave her the Tahiti's schedule." He squinted. "She said she'd need to find another way. That the Tahiti was — she used this word — too visible." He tapped his pipe against the counter edge. "I didn't know ships could be visible or otherwise. But she seemed to mean it as a fact."

Benton absorbed this. The Tahiti was the obvious Strait crossing — the most well-known vessel on the run, first class and second, passenger manifest submitted through official harbour channels. If Pid believed she was being watched — or that the ship itself was being watched — she would need something less documented. A private berth on a cargo vessel, or a fishing run, or nothing scheduled at all. Wherever she had gone, she had not gone plainly.

"You ever think of taking a trip yourself?" Charlie said, watching him. "You've got the look of a man who needs a horizon, Benton. The Tahiti's sailing end of the week — Canterbury wool charter, one-off, she won't be on the Strait again once she's gone south for refit. First class, second class — very civilised. A transpacific liner doing the Strait crossing, that's not something you see twice."

Benton set the last stack on the counter and picked up the empty crate. The Tahiti was not a horizon. It was the only direction he had.

"I'll bear it in mind," he said.

He stepped out into the afternoon. He stood on the footpath a moment, the envelope in his coat pocket, the press of the lunchtime crowd moving around him, thinking not about the ship as a holiday but about a dark-haired woman asking quietly for a passage south three days before anyone had registered her missing.

If that was Pid — and some cold, instinctive part of him was already past the if — then she had not simply wandered out of the hospital in a daze. She had known where she was going. She had been going there deliberately.

The question was why she hadn't told him.

He needed Marcus. And he needed whatever answers the hospital still had left to give.

He returned to the office in the early evening with the empty delivery crate and Mr. Cain's truck keys and a head full of arithmetic that refused to resolve. The print shop was locked and dark; the Cains had gone home. He let himself in through the side door, went upstairs, and sat at his desk in the yellow lamplight with nowhere particular to be and no particular reason not to stay there.

The evening edition of the Dominion was on the mat where it had been slid under the door. He did not read it. He knew what it would contain: parliament, commodities, sport, shipping movements, the managed version of the world. He had been compiling his own version of the world for a year now and the two versions shared very little territory.

He picked up his pen. Put it down.

On his desk was the photograph — already committed to this issue, now circulating across the city in stacks of fifty. He looked at it. The figure on the ground, the dark fluid pooling beneath it, the quality of the visible skin. He had looked at this photograph two hundred times in the course of setting the layout and it had not grown less strange with repetition. It had grown more so. The features of the figure — the absence of identifiable human feature, the distribution of the joints at angles no articulated skeleton could produce — presented the same information each time and each time his mind refused the information in the same way, like a file key that almost fits. He set the photograph face-down on the desk.

The issuing office name at the top had been removed in a clean horizontal strip, done with a ruler or straight edge, not in haste. In the authorisation box, dense official language: the bearer is authorised to act in the interests of the Crown in matters pertaining to the identified articles. Date stamp: Wellington, six days prior. Signatory initials at the lower right corner: G.M.

He folded the document and put it in his inner breast pocket.

The porthole on the port side was latched from inside. He undid it, looked through at the water thirty feet below, then closed it again.

He straightened the desk. He let himself out.

He was two steps down the corridor before the porthole registered properly. He stopped.

It had been latched from the inside.

A man going out through a porthole thirty feet above open water cannot latch it behind him. Which meant either Pelham had not gone out through it at all — and someone else had opened it and relatched it to suggest he had — or something had come in through it and latched it after. Both interpretations were available. Neither was an improvement on the other.

A Crown transit document with its issuing office surgically removed. G.M. Initials that meant nothing yet — he put them in the same mental room as Stanhope's grey motor car, as the grey-bearded man who had bought his entire Wellington print run, as the padlock on Marcus's deed-box that had been opened and replaced. The room that had no label on it yet, the one he circled and approached from different angles without being able to find the door in.

He went down to the lower decks because he had not yet been there and the cataloguing was incomplete. Steerage was a long forward compartment with tiered bunks, a low ceiling, and the particular density of air that accumulates when too many people have occupied a space for too long. It was noisy and warm and smelled of tobacco and cooking from the small adjacent galley. Most of the steerage passengers were working men, travelling alone or in groups of two, heading south for the goldfields at Reefton or the timber country further in. He passed through it and followed the forward passage to the outer hull.

The narrow external walkway ran along the lower hull near the forward mooring bollards — maintenance access, not passenger territory. He had been along it once at boarding to satisfy himself it existed. He went along it now because it was the last section of the ship's exterior he had not checked since sailing.

He stopped where the walkway met the mooring platform and leaned out over the rail. The hull dropped away below him — black paint, riveted plate, the Plimsoll line, and below that the waterline where the hull met the Strait. It was from this angle, looking down along the hull's exterior face, that he saw it.

A long horizontal score in the black paint at the waterline, very straight, very even-pressured — not the kind of mark made by another vessel or by dock contact. More like the kind of mark made by something running along the hull at speed with something very hard, tracing the ship's full length at exactly the depth where the steel met the water.

He crouched to look at it. Hemi Te Oka was already there, crouching at the hull's edge, and looked up without surprise — the look of a man who had been expecting the walk to bring someone eventually.

"One missing from first class," Benton said.

Hemi stood. He looked at the mark for another moment, then at the water below it, which was dark and showed nothing.

"It's sounding us," Hemi said. "Finding the shape of us." He looked at Benton for a moment, then at the companionway door behind them. "There's something in the engine room you should see."

Two decks below the passenger accommodation the air changed entirely: the ship's ambient cold-salt replaced by dense machine heat, the smell of coal and spent oil, the dry industrial warmth of sustained combustion. The sound changed too — the upper-deck noises of a passenger ship gave way to the unmediated fact of an engine working at close range, the massive reciprocating pistons audible as individual events rather than a collective background. Gauge panels attended by two men in leather aprons. Heat that pressed on the face. Hemi moved through it with the ease of someone who had spent eight hours a day in it long enough to simply live inside it. He led Benton to the forward bulkhead — the inner hull plate below the waterline — and crouched. He placed his palm flat on the plating.

"Feel it."

Benton crouched and put his hand to the hull. Cold despite the room's heat, which was itself wrong — the plates were in proximity to the engine and should have absorbed its warmth. What came through the hull plate was different. A slower oscillation, lower, continuous — distributed through the metal the way a note is distributed through glass when a wet finger circles the rim. It was not varying. It was not produced by anything aboard.

"The stokers noticed it when the engine pressure equalised this morning," Hemi said, standing. "In the quiet between cycles, it was there. It has been there since we entered the deep water." She told me what her father's people knew of this water — not the Pākehā name for the Strait, the older name, the one it had before the separation of the islands. Benton said nothing. He looked at the hull plate.

"The islands were joined," Hemi continued, "until the sea came through. When it came through it brought what had lived below into proximity with the surface. The atua that Tangaroa holds within his realm — these are not new things and they are not small things and they have been known to us for the entirety of the time we have lived on these coasts above them." He held Benton's gaze steadily. "The crossing was always made with permission. Not as ceremony for its own sake — as a practice of knowing. You said to the water: I know what is here. I know this seam. I mean to pass and I mean no disturbance. And then you went, and the deep things knew you were of this land and that you understood your position relative to them." He paused. "The people on this ship didn't do that. They never do. Most crossings, nothing answers. It is deep and old and a ship on the surface is too small to register." He looked at the hull. "This crossing is different. What is aboard has woken it. And it is not angry — that word doesn't apply to something that old and that large. It is simply responding. The way the sea responds to anything that speaks clearly enough to reach it."

"What I have aboard," Benton said.

Hemi looked at him with the particular quality of a man who is not confirming but is not denying either — receiving information about what Benton knows, and filing it. "Whatever that old man upstairs proposes to do with it. Whatever you choose." He straightened. "I'm telling you this because my grandmother would have told you, if she were here, and she has been dead fifteen years and I am the nearest equivalent, and it seems to me you are the person aboard most likely to use the information." He started back toward the companionway. "The vibration in the plates has been increasing since noon. By tonight it will not be subtle."

Benton stayed a moment longer with his hand on the condenser pipe and the engine's heat on his face, and felt through his shoes the two vibrations: the manufactured mechanical pulse of the ship driving itself forward, and beneath it, through the steel of the hull and the cold of the Strait, the other one. Patient. Continuous. As impersonal as geology.

He went back up.

The passenger world received him in the way it received anyone returning from the below-decks — with complete indifference, with the sounds of card games in progress and the smell of the saloon's afternoon tea service and two children being negotiated past the bottom of the main staircase by a woman who was failing to keep her voice below the volume that achieved results. It was perfectly ordinary. On the promenade, through the door, the sky was its flat grey afternoon self and the water was the Strait being the Strait — a body of water with a specific meteorological character that Benton had crossed twelve times and which had never before struck him as the surface of something.

He went to his cabin. He sat on the bunk with his coat on and did what he did with information he couldn't immediately structure: ran it from the beginning. Hemi Te Oka had told him two separate things. The first was cosmological — the entity's relationship to the Strait, the Māori understanding of it as a seam between states, the practice of asking permission as a practice of knowledge rather than ceremony. This was consistent with Voss's account, and consistent with the vibration in the hull, and consistent with the particular quality of the water since they'd entered the deep channel. It did not require Benton to accept it literally to find it useful. He filed it as local knowledge of a specific body of water, held by people who had been navigating it for longer than the chart-making tradition Voss had cited.

The second thing was personal and could not be filed the same way. What is aboard has woken it. He had understood, in the directness of Hemi's gaze when he said it, that what is aboard was not solely the Book of Abzu — that it was also him, specifically, him in conjunction with the book, and that Hemi had registered this the same way Voss had registered it and Marcus had been registering it for eleven months, and that the sum of these independent observations now constituted something he was having considerable difficulty not calling evidence.

The journalist standard for evidence was this: multiple sources, independent of each other, who could not have colluded, arriving at the same structural conclusion from different directions.

He sat with the implications of this for a while, and then got up and washed his face, and went to dinner.

Chapter Chapter 10

He went back to Voss that evening.

He found him in the first-class smoking lounge after dinner, alone with a brandy and documents spread across the small table before him — papers covered in dense script, diagrams, what appeared to be a section of nautical chart annotated in red ink. Voss covered none of it when Benton sat down. Either he had expected the return or he had decided covering things would serve no further purpose.

"The mark on the hull," Benton said. "Port side, near the waterline. Forward. You've seen it."

"I saw it when we sailed," Voss said. "It was there before the Pelham man went overboard — before he was taken, more accurately." He passed the nautical chart across. It showed the Cook Strait in fine detail, the deep water between the two islands marked with soundings. In the deepest section — northeast of Tory Channel entrance, where the Strait narrowed to some seventeen miles across — Voss had marked a circle in red. Inside the circle, two crossed lines.

"This is where it has rested," Voss said, tapping the circle. "The chart is based on hydrological surveys from the 1880s that noted anomalous sonar returns at depths beyond instrument range. The Māori have a name for the particular stretch of water. They do not fish it. They do not sail it at night if they can avoid it." He produced a second sheet. "This is a passage from the Book of Abzu, transcribed before the book was relocated from Vienna in 1891 and subsequently lost. It took me thirty-one years to find it again." He pointed to a section. "The phrase here, in the older Sumerian register, translates approximately as it sleeps at the seam. The Cook Strait is geologically a seam — the boundary between two tectonic plates. The entity has been living at that boundary since before human existence."

Benton looked at the transcription. He could not read Sumerian. But looking at the text he felt the beginning of something — not comprehension, but familiarity, the horrible kind, the kind that suggests you have read something before that you know you have not.

He pressed his fingers against the scar on his shoulder and looked up. "What did you do to get Holbrook to leave. And where is Pid Gus."

Voss regarded him with something that was not quite respect and not quite regret. "Holbrook was a pragmatic man. "She's not on this ship."

"No. I checked the manifest before sailing. She is not." Voss folded the transcription carefully and returned it to its folder. "She is not what I need. You are. "Your plan," Benton said, very carefully, "is to use me as the mechanism of a ritual to bind an ancient oceanic creature to your will."

"To contain it. Not to destroy it — that is not possible. To establish a constraint." Voss picked up his brandy. "Without your cooperation the binding will not function. The entity will surface, complete its reconnection to the signal the book emits, and recede. Some deaths will occur in the process. Proximity deaths — people in the wrong place. It will not pursue." He met Benton's eyes. "With the binding: we constrain it, no one dies, and I spend the remaining years of my professional life ensuring no one is foolish enough to broadcast that frequency again." He sipped his brandy. "That is the proposition."

Benton remained seated. He had the question he always had at this stage of an investigation — the one that separated the structure from the facts — and he asked it the way he always asked it.

"Who is the Conclave," he said. "Not what it wants. Who it is."

Voss appeared unsurprised by the question, which meant he had anticipated it and had prepared a version of the answer. He set his glass down. "Founded in Vienna in 1412, under the name Collegium Meridiani — the College of the Meridian, after an astronomical metaphor for the point of passage between one celestial hemisphere and another. Its founders were scholars who had assembled, from various Arabic and pre-Greek sources, enough of the pre-deluge record to understand that certain ancient texts were not metaphorical accounts of natural phenomena but transcriptions of actual communication with non-human intelligences — an understanding that took the next three hundred years of scholarship to make operational, and another two hundred to lose, and which we have spent the last century attempting to recover." He said this with the measured pride of a man describing an institution's history in a way that does not quite conceal how much of his own life has been constituted by it. "At its peak, approximately two hundred members across six European cities and two in the Americas. Currently: eleven active researchers, of whom I am one, working from what remains of the Vienna archive following the war's redistribution of institutional memory." He paused. "Our mandate, if you require the word, is documentation and containment. We do not summon. We have never summoned. What happened under Te Aro was not the Conclave — it was an amateur application of Conclave-derived knowledge by a provincial society that misunderstood the basic nature of what it was working with. A man with a map who does not know what the contour lines mean."

Benton sat with this. It was, as professional briefs went, thorough — the historical grounding, the institutional context, the implicit authority claim. It was also the account that a genuine eleven-hundred-year-old scholarly society would give of itself, and simultaneously the account a well-resourced fraud would give. He had been given both kinds in his career and they were indistinguishable from each other in the telling.

"You said thirty-one years to find the book," Benton said. "Vienna to Wellington."

"Twenty-eight years searching. Three years knowing." Voss turned his brandy glass. "A man named Alois sold the book from the library of the Society's Vienna chapter in 1891, during a period of institutional disagreement about the appropriate management of the more volatile acquisitions. He sold it to an antiquities dealer in Hamburg. The Hamburg dealer sold it to a collector in Rotterdam, who died in 1908 and whose estate was partly acquired by a London house and partly dispersed by private arrangement. The private arrangement included a certain volume described in Rotterdam's estate catalogue as Book, hand-written, language uncertain, boards worn, minimal monetary value. That description appeared in the London house's auction records in 1911. It was purchased by a New Zealander on an academic visit." He paused. "It took me until 1921 to establish who. It took me a further year to establish that the purchaser had died in 1916 and the book had passed to a colleague of his at the Wellington museum, and that the colleague had divested it before the Te Aro incident through a private sale to a bookseller." He paused. "The bookseller sold it in turn to the chairman of the Wellington Philosophical Society — a man named Wendell Carth. Carth brought it to two academics he had recruited to the Society for the purpose of translation. A husband and wife, originally from Austro-Hungary. Their name was Baláž." He held Benton's gaze. "I expect you know what followed from that." He looked at the table for a moment. "Thirty-one years."

Benton looked at the table for a moment. "The man at the top of the first-class companionway when we sailed," he said. "Grey beard, trimmed to a point. Long dark overcoat. Speaking with the purser." He said it without particular emphasis, the way a man names something he has carried in his notes for forty hours and is only now producing. "That isn't you."

A pause. Something in Voss's expression changed — not the considered recalibration he had shown when Benton used his name at the outset. Something involuntary. A brief arrest, the kind that happens when a man receives information that revises something he had considered settled.

"No," Voss said.

"Is he Conclave."

"No." Without hesitation.

Benton let the silence sit for two seconds. "Then you have a problem with your count."

Voss looked at him. For the first time in the conversation he had no immediate answer. His hand found the ring and stopped. "I saw that man this morning on the first-class promenade," he said quietly. "I had assumed he was attached to the Crown function. The manner." He paused. "If he is not—"

"If he is not," Benton said, "then someone has known about this crossing long enough to book first-class passage and brief the ship's purser before we left harbour. And has been aboard for ten hours without saying anything to you about it."

Voss turned the ring once, slowly. He looked at the chart — not at the marked circle but at the open water between it and the edges of the two islands, the unnamed depth.

Benton stood up.

"I'll need until morning," he said.

He did not look back. Voss was still looking at the chart when the smoking lounge door closed behind him.

Marsh was in the service corridor.

He was standing at the junction where the starboard passage met the companionway to the upper decks, with no particular alibi for the location — simply occupying the junction with the patient completeness of someone who expected to be found there. His hands were in his coat pockets and he gave off the particular quality of a man who has been operating professionally long enough that his own stillness is one of his instruments.

Up close and outside the dining saloon's forgiving light, Marsh resolved into something more specific: middle fifties, medium height, the build of a man who had once been lean and had since arrived at a compact, contained solidity. The precisely maintained beard was brown going grey at the jaw. His eyes were a pale, undemonstrative colour — grey or green, the service corridor lamplight not settling the question — and held the particular quality of eyes that have spent a long time watching and have stopped needing to show that they are doing it.

"Mr. Bright," he said.

Benton stopped. He had not seen him until six feet away. "Mr. Marsh. The wool market keeps unusual hours."

Something crossed Marsh's face that acknowledged the line without responding to it. He moved slightly to allow notional room in the corridor. "I'll be straightforward. I have limited time and I suspect you do too." He held Benton's gaze with the focused patience of a man who has assessed whether the person across from him is worth spending candour on and has arrived at a provisional yes. "Gideon Marsh. I represent an interest of the Crown — not the police, not the civil service as it presents itself publicly. A separate function." A pause. "You are carrying an ancient text. It was in a bookshop on Cuba Street until three days ago and it is now on this ship."

"If I had such a thing," Benton said.

"You do," Marsh said, simply. "Holbrook's report named it before his departure was arranged. We have known of the book's existence since the Te Aro incident. Locating it took considerably longer." He paused. "The man in first class — Voss — has made you a proposition. I don't know its exact terms. I know what he wants to use the book for, and I am here to tell you that the Crown has an interest in ensuring that use does not proceed without oversight."

"The Crown," Benton said carefully, "has spent the last year ensuring nothing about what happened under Te Aro is public knowledge."

Marsh absorbed this without any change of expression. "Different offices, different instructions. The people who managed the public record of that incident have no knowledge of this operation. This is older, and comes from further up." He folded his hands. He produced no credentials. Nothing official changed hands. "Whatever Voss proposes — any use of that text in proximity to what lies below this vessel — the book is an artefact of strategic significance and its employment cannot be delegated to a private Continental archaeologist with no Crown mandate."

"What is your plan," Benton said.

"On arrival at Lyttelton, the book transfers to official custody. That is all that is required from you. Nothing tonight. No procedure, no confrontation. When we dock, you come to me with the box and the matter is closed from your perspective." He allowed a measured pause. "The ministry inquiry into your licensing — Stanhope answers to me in this matter, and that conversation ends when the book is in custody. Your friend Spöring's name is on no official documentation as of today. I can keep it that way." He held Benton's gaze. "If you proceed with Voss instead, the Crown manages the aftermath. Including what you were doing in a dead passenger's cabin this afternoon."

Benton looked at him steadily. "Pelham worked for you."

"He did," Marsh said. Flat. Two words, no elaboration — a eulogy that wouldn't run to three. "His loss changes my position aboard considerably. It doesn't change what I'm asking." He straightened. "Do nothing tonight. In the morning, before we dock, come to my cabin. Second class, fourteen, portside."

He turned to go.

"Why should I trust your plan above Voss's?" Benton said.

Marsh stopped. He looked back once. "You shouldn't, particularly," he said. "But you should understand that when I say the book's use cannot proceed unsupervised, I am not saying it because I want to own the outcome. Those are different positions." He paused. "Voss has told you the entity can be constrained. That is what Voss believes. The Crown's information on this subject is older than Voss's career. The accounting was this. He was basing the scale on Te Aro, which he now suspected was not the right basis. What had been in the cave under Te Aro had been a contact point, a thread of something reaching upward. What was below the hull of this ship was the thing the thread was attached to.

And Marcus, who was a fourth party, who wanted to throw the book into the Strait, which was the only proposition that did not require Benton to understand what the book actually was before he gave it up.

That was the problem. That was the specific quality of his difficulty, arrived at now, in this corridor, with Marsh's footsteps already out of earshot: he did not know what the book actually was. He had opened it. He had read it once, quickly, in the passenger seat of a closed roadster on the road to Aro Street, Pid at the wheel and Marcus bleeding in the back — which was not the same as having read it. He had been told things about it by Voss, who believed them; by Marcus, who had held it eleven months and wanted it at the bottom of the sea; by Marsh, who had produced a document suggesting the Crown wanted custody of something its own operative had apparently died to learn the value of. He had seen it work once, in the cave, in the hands of a man whose hands he had drawn the seal on in blood while conscious of almost nothing, having made a prior decision that it was right. He had been told — by inference, by Hemi's cosmology, by the recurring dreams, by the waking hallucination with Marcus's face, by every accumulated signal he had been receiving for eleven months — that it had already begun changing him. That it had been changing him since the first time he touched it.

And he did not know what it was.

He could not give it to Marsh without knowing what binding is not the correct word meant. He could not give it to Voss without knowing whether the containment would work or what the cost of it would be. He could not throw it overboard without knowing whether that made things better or merely lost the option of making them better later.

Any responsible editor refused to run a story he had not finished reading.

He went back to his cabin.

Chapter Epilogue

The cliff above Te Aro faced east, which meant it caught the full weight of the storm coming in off the Strait without any obstruction between the weather and itself.

The woman on it did not shelter.

She stood at the edge with one boot forward on the rock, weight distributed in the automatic way of someone who has spent a long time in country that does not cooperate with the ordinary ambitions of balance. The storm moved through her — rain horizontal and cold, the harbour below invisible behind it, the city reduced to amber smears of lamplight in the grey — and she received it as information rather than assault. Her canvas coat had been well-made once. It was now the coat of someone who had put it through considerable use in conditions that were not temperate. The shoulders were worn to the liner. The hem had been recut and restitched with a larger gauge, practically done by someone who was not concerned with appearance and had been in a place where a seamstress was not available.

Her boots reached the knee. Good leather, dark with rain, with the stiff articulated ankle of boots designed for rough ground and which had clearly negotiated a substantial quantity of it. They were a distinctive make — formal enough for a city, durable enough for what lay beyond one. The kind of thing a woman wore when she was not prepared to be limited by terrain.

She carried a rifle across both forearms in the carrying grip of a person for whom the weapon's weight had become familiar over a long time. It was not a standard colonial piece — not the sort that came out of an Auckland gun shop for rabbits and deer. The barrel was longer than the conventional pattern, the receiver housing differently weighted, the whole instrument giving the impression of a thing that had been considered and then altered, by someone with a specific problem in mind. Along the barrel and across the grip plate, worked in shallow relief into the metal itself: a repeating pattern of overlapping forms — the arced segments of deep-sea carapaces, the scroll of a shell, the articulated geometry of things that live below the photosynthesis line, rendered with a jeweller's precision into surfaces that the rain ran across without illumining. It was not decorative. Or rather, it was decorative in the way that marks of knowledge are decorative — intentional, specific, placed by someone who understood what they were referencing.

Below her, across the wet rooflines of Te Aro, one window was lit.

She had been watching it for some time. The rain had reached the lining of her coat awhile ago and settled into it, and the cold was simply present now — a standing condition, not an event. She watched the window the way someone watches a thing they have already arrived at conclusions about: not waiting, because for her the uncertainty was not whether but how far, and that question could not be answered at this distance, and perhaps not at any.

The light in the window was steady and pale. A lamp keeping company with someone at work. It had the quality of a light left on past the point where sleep should have intervened, and it had not moved since she arrived at the cliff's edge. The kind of light that suggests an appetite that sleep cannot address. She knew that appetite. She had seen it developing in him before she left, and she had known then what she was watching, and she had made a calculation she was not prepared to review at this distance in this weather.

She knew the room behind that window. She had stood in it. Drunk tea in it. Argued with the man at the desk about what the public had a right to know and been told, in the patient and slightly condescending way of journalists, that the answer to that question was everything and the follow-up question of whether they were ready for it was someone else's concern.

She had known, by the end, that he was wrong about that. She had known it before she left Wellington, and she had spent the months since acquiring evidence for a position she had held on instinct before the evidence existed. The evidence was, it turned out, substantially worse than the instinct.

The storm pressed across the cliff and the city and the harbour without interest in any of it. The crustacean forms on the rifle's receiver held no light. The lights of Te Aro below were gauze and diffusion behind the rain.

The window shifted — not extinguished, but moved within. A lamp being carried to a different part of the room, or an occupant repositioning, the light-source and its shadow briefly separated before settling again. She tracked the change with eyes that had spent months in conditions where changes of this kind were the only available information, and the habit of reading them had become the same as the habit of breathing: continuous, unconscious, and not easy to stop.

The light stilled. Whatever was in that room had settled.

She did not move toward it.

She had not moved toward it for three hours. The decision not to was not ambivalence — she had ceased being ambivalent about most things some weeks prior, in circumstances she had no intention of revisiting before she had slept, and possibly not then. The decision was tactical. She was still becoming clear on the shape of the ground. That was the honest version.

After a while the storm thickened and the window reduced further, from a window to a pale smear to a warm suggestion behind the grey. She watched it for another moment. Then she lifted the rifle from the carrying position, settled it to her shoulder for a breath — not aiming, only checking the line of sight, the habit of a person who has learned not to trust distance and wishes to know the correction — and then lowered it again.

She turned.

She went back the way she had come, into the dark above the city, and the storm covered her tracks as she went, the way storms do, as though the ground had decided to keep the question open.
